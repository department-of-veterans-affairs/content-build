# Help with VA Form 10-0137 — VA Advance Directive

---

## About the form

### What is a VA Advance Directive?

A VA Advance Directive is a legal document that tells VA your health
care wishes before an emergency happens. It has two parts:

1. **Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care** — You name a trusted
   person (a "Health Care Agent") to make health care decisions for
   you if you become unable to make them yourself.

2. **Living Will** — You describe the medical treatments you do or
   don't want in specific situations, such as if you are in a coma,
   have severe dementia, or have a terminal illness.

You don't have to complete both parts. You can fill out only the part
that applies to you.

---

### Why should I complete an advance directive?

Completing this form ensures your health care wishes are known and
documented in your VA health record. If you have a serious illness,
are injured, or can no longer speak for yourself, your care team and
loved ones will know what you want.

Without an advance directive, the people caring for you may not know
your preferences, and decisions may need to be made without your
guidance.

---

### Is the VA Advance Directive the same as a state advance directive?

No. VA Form 10-0137 is designed specifically for VA health care
facilities. It is valid at all VA facilities **without notarization**.

If you want your advance directive to be legally binding **outside VA**
— for example, at a community hospital or emergency room — you may
also need a state-specific advance directive that meets your state's
legal requirements. Talk to a legal advisor or your state's health
department for more information.

---

### Does this form expire?

No. Your advance directive stays in your VA health record until you
update or revoke it. A newer version always replaces an older one.

---

## Completing the form online

### Do I need to sign in to complete this form?

Yes. You must sign in with a verified VA.gov account
(Login.gov or ID.me) to complete this form online. This protects
your sensitive health information.

If you don't have a verified account,
[learn how to verify your identity on VA.gov](/resources/signing-in-to-vagov/).

---

### Can I save my progress and come back later?

Yes. Your progress is saved automatically while you're signed in.
If you need to stop, select **Save and finish later**. When you
return, sign in and select **Continue saved form**.

---

### My personal information looks wrong. What should I do?

Your name, date of birth, address, and phone number are pre-filled
from your VA profile. If the information is outdated, you can:

- Update it on this form for this advance directive only, **or**
- [Update your VA profile](https://www.va.gov/profile/) so it stays
  current for all VA records

If your name or date of birth is incorrect in your VA record, contact
your VA facility's enrollment office.

---

### Do I have to answer every question?

No. You may complete some, all, or none of the Living Will sections.
There is no penalty for leaving sections blank. Your care team and
Health Care Agent will use whatever information you do provide.

The only sections you must complete are:
- **Part I** — Your personal information
- **Part IV** — Your digital signature and two witness signatures

---

### What does it mean to name a Health Care Agent?

Your Health Care Agent is someone you trust to make health care
decisions for you if you become unable to make them yourself — for
example, if you are unconscious or have severe dementia.

Your Health Care Agent can:
- Agree to or refuse treatments on your behalf
- Access your medical records to make informed decisions
- Make decisions about where you receive care

Your Health Care Agent should know your values and what matters most
to you. Talk to them before you name them so they understand and
accept this responsibility.

---

### Who can be my Health Care Agent?

You can name any trusted adult — a spouse, partner, family member,
or close friend. There are no legal restrictions on who can serve
as your Health Care Agent under VA's form, but you should choose
someone who:

- Knows your values and health care wishes
- Will be available and reachable in an emergency
- Is willing to advocate for you even under pressure

Your Health Care Agent **cannot** also serve as one of your two
witnesses.

---

### What are the six medical situations in the Living Will section?

The Living Will asks about your preferences in six defined situations:

1. You are unconscious, in a coma, or in a vegetative state with
   little or no chance of recovery
2. You have permanent, severe brain damage and cannot recognize your
   family or friends (for example, severe dementia)
3. You need permanent help from others for your daily activities
   (such as eating, bathing, and toileting)
4. You need a breathing machine and would be confined to bed
   for the rest of your life
5. You have pain or severe symptoms that cause suffering and
   cannot be relieved
6. You have a condition that will cause you to die very soon, even
   with life-sustaining treatments

For each situation, you can say you **want** life-sustaining treatments,
you're **not sure**, or you **do not want** them. You can also skip
any situation you don't want to answer.

---

### What are life-sustaining treatments?

Life-sustaining treatments are medical procedures that keep you alive
but don't cure your condition. Examples include:

- **CPR (cardiopulmonary resuscitation)** — chest compressions and
  rescue breathing to restart the heart
- **Mechanical ventilation (breathing machine)** — a machine that
  breathes for you when you can't breathe on your own
- **Feeding tube (artificial nutrition and hydration)** — nutrients
  and fluids delivered through a tube when you can't eat or drink
- **Kidney dialysis** — a machine that filters your blood when your
  kidneys can no longer do so

## Witness requirements

### Why do I need two witnesses?

Two witnesses protect the integrity of your advance directive. They
confirm that you signed the form voluntarily and that you understood
what you were signing.

---

### Who can serve as a witness?

A witness must personally observe you completing and signing the form.

A witness **cannot** be:
- The person you named as your Health Care Agent in this form
- Anyone named as a beneficiary in your estate (such as in a will
  or trust)
- Anyone who is financially responsible for your care
- Anyone who signed the form on your behalf at your direction

---

### Can my spouse or family member be a witness?

Yes, as long as they are not named as your Health Care Agent in this
form and do not meet any of the other disqualifier conditions listed
above. However, if you also named them as a beneficiary in your estate,
they cannot serve as a witness.

---

### What if I can't find two qualifying witnesses?

Contact your VA facility's patient advocate or social worker. They
can help arrange witnesses or connect you with staff who can assist
you in completing the form.

---

## After you submit

### How will I know my form was received?

After you submit, you'll see a confirmation page with a reference
number. You'll also receive a confirmation email. Your advance
directive will be sent to your VA care team and added to your VA
health record within **3 business days**.

---

### Will my VA care team read my advance directive?

Your care team will be notified when you submit your form. Your
advance directive will be part of your VA health record. It's a good
idea to also talk to your primary care provider about your wishes at
your next appointment so they are aware of your preferences.

---

### How do I update my advance directive?

You can complete and submit a new VA Form 10-0137 at any time at
[va.gov/health-care/advance-directive](/health-care/advance-directive/).
A newer form automatically replaces the older one in your VA health
record.

---

### How do I revoke (cancel) my advance directive?

You can revoke your advance directive at any time by:

- **Submitting a new form** — A new advance directive replaces the
  previous one
- **Telling a VA clinician in person** — You can verbally tell your
  VA care team you want to revoke your advance directive. Ask them
  to document this in your health record.
- **Writing a signed statement** — A written, signed statement of
  revocation given to your VA care team also cancels the directive.

---

### Can I download a copy of my completed form?

Yes. After you submit, you can download or print a PDF copy from the
confirmation page. We recommend keeping a copy for your own records
and sharing a copy with your Health Care Agent.

## Get help

### Talk to someone at VA

**Call MyVA411:** [1-800-698-2411](tel:18006982411)
(TTY: [711](tel:711))
Available Monday–Friday, 8:00 a.m. to 8:00 p.m. ET

**VA online chat:** [va.gov/contact-us](/contact-us/)

### Talk to your VA care team

Your primary care provider, social worker, patient advocate, or
palliative care team can help you think through your health care
wishes and complete this form. Ask at your next appointment or
send a secure message through
[My HealtheVet](https://www.myhealth.va.gov/).

### Visit your VA facility

Any VA Medical Center or clinic can help you complete your advance
directive in person. Ask for the patient advocate at the front desk.
